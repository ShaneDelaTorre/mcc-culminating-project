Chapter One
Know
Yourself
ENFJ — the result that caught me off guard

I never thought of myself as an extrovert — but I also never fully felt like an introvert. So seeing ENFJ on the screen was genuinely surprising. It made me pause and think: is this actually me? The more I read, the more it clicked — not as a perfect definition, but as a useful mirror.

"The test didn't tell me who I am. It gave me a language for things I already was — and a map for the parts I hadn't noticed yet."

Strengths to use

Connecting with people, reading the room, and genuinely caring about the team — these showed up naturally and made collaboration feel easier than expected.

Weaknesses to watch

Absorbing the team's stress as my own. Learning to notice that pattern — and not let the room's energy override my own clarity — was a quiet but important shift.

What awareness gave me
  • 1 I stopped guessing why I react certain ways — I could name it and work with it.
  • 2 Knowing your strengths means you can use them on purpose, not by accident.
  • 3 Knowing your weaknesses means you can catch yourself before they catch you.
If I were starting over —

Take the test early and sit with the results honestly. Don't just read the strengths. The weaknesses section is where the real self-awareness lives.

Chapter Two
People
Skills
Where I struggled

Whenever I was given a task or instructions, my brain would jump straight to solving — and I'd miss half of what was actually being said. I was actively listening and actively thinking at the same time, and thinking was winning. I had to learn to slow down and fully receive before I responded.

"A solution built on half an instruction is twice the work. Listen completely first."

Vague

"Yeah I'll work on it"
"Almost done"
"Should be fine"

→
Clear

"Done by 5pm"
"Blocked on X — need help"
"Here's what I decided and why"

The link to better code

Clearer communication meant fewer re-dos. When I fully understood the task before starting, I wrote more focused code, asked better questions, and delivered closer to what was actually needed — not just what I thought was needed.

Rules I kept from Celeste Headlee
Listen to understand Ask, don't assume Be brief, be clear Stay present Don't multitask while listening
If I were starting over —

When being given instructions, write them down in real time. It forces you to listen fully and gives you something to confirm back before you start.

Culture
What I appreciate

The SSED team's generosity was consistent and genuine — always willing to share tools, ideas, knowledge, and their time without being asked. That kind of culture isn't written in a handbook. It's shown by people, every day.

Core OpsWerks methods that stuck
  • 1Make it meaningful. Not every day is a highlight — most of it is the middle. Finding meaning in ordinary moments made the work feel purposeful, not just productive.
  • 2Grow and go. Every season has something to teach. What you absorb here propels you forward — carry the learning, not just the output.
My seasons here
Arrived
Uncertain but open, watching how people moved and worked.
Learned
Started understanding the culture — not just the tools but the people behind them.
Applied
Put those learnings into practice — in my code, my communication, and how I showed up.
Propelled
Ready to carry this season's wisdom into whatever comes next.
If I were starting over —

Pay attention to how people around you work, not just what they build. The culture is in the small moments — how someone answers a question, how a team handles a mistake.

Chapter Four
Growth
Mind
set
Where I struggled with this the most

There were moments I felt like everyone around me was already so far ahead — and that gap felt real. The instinct was to sit with that feeling quietly. But growth meant doing something with it instead.

"Being far behind doesn't mean failing. It means there's so much more to gain — and the people ahead of you are the best resource in the room."

Accepting it
  • ✕ I don't belong here
  • ✕ Everyone's better than me
  • ✕ I'm not good enough at X or Y
→
Doing something about it
  • ✓ I'm exactly where I should be
  • ✓ They're who I'm here to learn from
  • ✓ I'm weak at X and Y — so I'll learn X and Y
The shift

I stopped seeing my peers as proof of my shortcomings — and started seeing them as the most valuable resource in the room. Inspiration, not comparison.

If I were starting over —

When you feel behind, write down specifically what you feel behind on. Vague inadequacy is paralyzing. A named skill gap is just a to-do item.
